How filters work — convolution, FIRs, biquads, and the time-frequency law

Every digital filter — the smoother on a sensor reading, the bass on a synth, the blur in Photoshop, the band-pass that finds your heartbeat — is the same operation: multiply past samples by weights, add them up. Here is how that one operation gives rise to FIR and IIR, why narrow-band filters always ring, and why the same machinery filters a sound and an image without changing a line.

§ 1

The simplest filter: running average

A signal, for our purposes, is just an array of numbers — measurements taken at regular intervals. Audio is a signal. A breath sensor's output is a signal. Even a row of pixels across an image is a signal.

A filter is a function that takes one signal and produces another, sample by sample. The simplest one is the running average: each output sample is the mean of the last few input samples.

def running_average(x, N):
    out = [0] * len(x)
    for i in range(len(x)):
        # window of last N samples (or as many as we have)
        lo = max(0, i - N + 1)
        out[i] = sum(x[lo : i + 1]) / N
    return out

Drag the kernel-length slider. The orange trace is the input — a sine wave with noise added. The teal trace is the output. Notice that as N grows, the noise wiggles get smaller (the filter smooths) but the curve also lags slightly behind the input. Both effects are inevitable. They are the whole subject of this page.

As you can see, the running average is an unweighted sum — every sample in the window contributes equally. That uniformity has a cost we will diagnose in Section 4: the running average attenuates some frequencies less than others, in a pattern that turns out to be embarrassingly bad. But the structure of the operation — slide a window, multiply each sample by a weight, add — is the same structure every digital filter on this page uses.

§ 2

Convolution — kernel, slide, multiply, sum

Generalising the running average is simple: replace the equal weights with arbitrary weights. The list of weights is called the kernel (or impulse response, or filter taps — three names for the same array). The operation that applies the kernel to a signal is called convolution.

def convolve(x, h):
    # h is the kernel (length M); x is the input (length N)
    out = [0.0] * len(x)
    M = len(h)
    for i in range(len(x)):
        for k in range(M):
            if i - k >= 0:
                out[i] += h[k] * x[i - k]
    return out

That inner loop is the entire engine of digital filtering. Every FIR filter on this page is exactly this loop with a different kernel h. The kernel has length M, and the output at sample i is a weighted sum of the most recent M input samples.

As you can see, the kernel shape is the filter's behaviour. The triangular kernel weights the centre sample most heavily and gives a smoother output than the rectangular running average. The two-tap difference [1, -1] is the simplest high-pass filter — it amplifies sample-to-sample changes and kills constants. The same loop, four different filters.

§ 3

The impulse response is the filter

What happens if we feed a kernel a signal that is zero everywhere except for a single 1 — an impulse? The convolution code multiplies every weight by zero except at the moment the impulse passes through, where it multiplies by 1. The output is a copy of the kernel, deposited at the impulse's location.

So the kernel is the filter's response to an impulse. We call it the impulse response, and it is the filter's complete identity: if you know the impulse response, you can predict the output for any input.

Click anywhere along the input row. You're placing an impulse there. The output row immediately shows the kernel stamped at that location. Click another spot — a second copy appears, and where the two stamps overlap, they add. That additivity is called linearity, and it's the property that makes filtering well-behaved.

Notice the third kernel option, "echo." It's a kernel of length four with a 1 at position 0 and a 0.6 at position 3. Convolved with any input, it produces the input plus a delayed, attenuated copy. That is a digital reverb. The whole audio-effects industry is just clever choices of impulse response.

§ 4

The same filter, frequency view

So far we've watched filters in the time domain — kernels stamping onto signals. The same filter has a second, equally-real existence in the frequency domain: a description of how much it attenuates or amplifies each input frequency.

The link between the two views is the discrete Fourier transform, which decomposes a signal (or a kernel) into a sum of pure sines and cosines. We'll use it as a tool, not derive it. The relevant fact: the magnitude of a kernel's Fourier transform, plotted against frequency, is its frequency response.

Drag the slider. On the left is the running-average kernel of length N. On the right is its frequency response: how loud each input frequency comes through. Notice the deep nulls. They sit at integer multiples of the sample rate divided by N.

nulls at f = k · fs / N for k = 1, 2, 3, … fs is the sample rate (44 100 Hz typical for audio); k indexes successive zeros.

That formula explains a tremendous amount of practical filter behaviour. A 5-sample running average at audio rate has nulls at 8 820 Hz, 17 640 Hz, and so on — so it kills those exact tones perfectly while letting their neighbours through almost unattenuated. The running average is, frequency-domain-wise, a strange filter: lumpy passband, lumpy stopband, deep nulls in awkward places. We almost always want better.

Figure 4 · time vs. frequencydrag N
kernel (left, time) frequency response (right, log magnitude)

As you slide N upward, the nulls in the frequency response march closer to zero (because fs/N shrinks) and the main lobe in the passband narrows. Longer kernel, narrower passband. That is the law we'll meet again and again. The next two sections design something better than the running average — but they cannot escape this law.

§ 5

Designing an FIR: the windowed sinc

If we want a clean low-pass — passes everything below some cutoff, kills everything above — there's a textbook recipe: take the inverse Fourier transform of the ideal "rectangle" frequency response. That gives a continuous function called the sinc.

import numpy as np

def windowed_sinc(cutoff, M, window='hann'):
    # cutoff in normalized freq (0..0.5); M = filter length
    n  = np.arange(M) - (M - 1) / 2
    h  = np.sinc(2 * cutoff * n)            # the ideal continuous filter
    if window == 'hann':
        h *= 0.5 - 0.5 * np.cos(2 * np.pi * np.arange(M) / (M - 1))
    return h / h.sum()                     # normalize DC gain to 1

An ideal sinc is infinitely long, which we cannot store. So we cut it off — a process called truncation — but a hard cut is itself a filter (a rectangular window) and it produces ringing in the frequency response, called the Gibbs phenomenon. The fix is to multiply the truncated sinc by a smoother window that tapers to zero at the edges. The Hann and Blackman windows are two such tapers.

Try the radio buttons. The rectangular window's frequency response has visible ripples in the stopband — about 21 dB of suppression, no matter how long you make the kernel. Hann gives ~44 dB of suppression with a gentler taper; Blackman gives ~74 dB but with a wider transition band. There's no free lunch — every choice of window trades sidelobe height against transition width.

Figure 5 · windowed sinctry each window
kernel frequency response (dB)

Notice the rect window's "shoulder" near the cutoff — small but persistent ripples in both passband and stopband. That's Gibbs. Hann smooths it dramatically. The kernel shape gets bumpier (literally has bumps in the middle taper) but the frequency response gets cleaner. The shape of the window is the price of the shape of the response.

§ 6

The tradeoff you cannot escape

Now we can put numbers on the central tradeoff. A windowed-sinc low-pass with kernel length M at sample rate fs has:

  • Transition width ≈ 4 · fs / M (Hann window). To make the transition twice as sharp, double the kernel length.
  • Latency = (M − 1) / 2 samples. The output is delayed by half the kernel length. Always.

Those two facts together mean: a sharper filter is always a slower filter, by exactly this proportion. Halving the transition band doubles the latency. There is no tap arrangement that escapes this.

§ 7

IIR — when recursion saves the day

Linear FIR filters are honest. You see exactly what they do (the kernel) and you can prove things about them (linear phase, guaranteed stability). But they are expensive: a 200-tap filter is 200 multiply-adds per output sample.

An IIR — infinite impulse response — filter sidesteps this by feeding its own output back into itself. Here are a one-pole low-pass and an FIR low-pass at the same cutoff frequency, side by side:

FIR (101 taps, Hann window)

def fir_lp(x, h):
    # h is 101 weights from §5
    out = [0.0] * len(x)
    M = len(h)
    for i in range(len(x)):
        for k in range(M):
            if i - k >= 0:
                out[i] += h[k] * x[i - k]
    return out
# 101 multiplies per sample

IIR (1 pole)

def iir_lp(x, alpha):
    # alpha = cutoff smoothing constant
    out = [0.0] * len(x)
    for i in range(len(x)):
        prev = out[i - 1] if i > 0 else 0
        out[i] = alpha * x[i] + (1 - alpha) * prev
    return out
# 2 multiplies per sample

The IIR's secret is that out[i] already contains the influence of every past input — through out[i-1], which contained out[i-2], and so on, all the way back to time zero. The recursion does in two multiplies what the FIR needs a hundred multiplies to do.

The figure compares both filters' frequency responses at the same cutoff. They are remarkably close at this scale. Slide the cutoff to compare them across the spectrum.

At low to mid cutoffs the curves agree to within a fraction of a decibel, and the IIR is 50× cheaper. The catch: an IIR has nonlinear phase — different frequencies pass through with different delays — which sometimes matters (in audio mastering, in certain measurement contexts) and sometimes doesn't (in real-time effects, sensor smoothing, image processing). For most jobs, IIR's cheapness wins.

§ 8

The biquad and the Q knob

The workhorse IIR filter is the biquad: two poles and two zeros, expressed as five coefficients. A single biquad can be a low-pass, a high-pass, a band-pass, a notch, a peaking EQ, a shelf — depending only on how you compute the coefficients. Robert Bristow-Johnson's "audio EQ cookbook" is the canonical recipe. Here it is, slightly simplified:

def biquad_bpf(f0, Q, fs):
    # band-pass biquad coefficients (RBJ cookbook)
    w0    = 2 * pi * f0 / fs
    alpha = sin(w0) / (2 * Q)
    cos_  = cos(w0)
    b0, b1, b2 =  alpha,  0,            -alpha
    a0, a1, a2 =  1 + alpha,  -2 * cos_,  1 - alpha
    return [c / a0 for c in (b0, b1, b2, a1, a2)]

def apply_biquad(x, b0, b1, b2, a1, a2):
    out = [0.0] * len(x)
    x1 = x2 = y1 = y2 = 0
    for i, xi in enumerate(x):
        yi = b0*xi + b1*x1 + b2*x2 - a1*y1 - a2*y2
        out[i] = yi
        x2, x1 = x1, xi
        y2, y1 = y1, yi
    return out

That's it — the entire IIR machinery for almost every audio EQ in the world. Q is the quality factor: the ratio of the centre frequency to the bandwidth. Low Q is a wide, gentle band; high Q is a tight, surgical resonance.

That's resonance — a band-pass with high Q is, audibly, a tuned bell.

§ 9

Stability — where filters explode

The IIR's recursion is what gives it cheapness, sharpness, and ringing. It is also what makes it dangerous. Feed an IIR's output back into itself with the wrong coefficients and the output grows without bound — the filter explodes.

The condition for stability has a clean geometric form. A biquad has two poles, complex numbers that determine its behaviour. Plot them on the complex plane, draw a circle of radius 1 (the unit circle). If both poles are inside the circle, the filter is stable: bounded input gives bounded output. If a pole reaches the circle, the filter rings forever (a sustained sinusoid). If a pole crosses outside, the output grows exponentially.

Drag the orange dot. We've placed one pole on the complex plane (its conjugate is automatically reflected — biquads with real coefficients always have conjugate-pair poles). The right panel shows the impulse response: the time-domain output when you feed the filter a single 1.

Inside the unit circle: the impulse response decays. Closer to the edge means slower decay (more ringing). On the circle: the impulse response oscillates forever at constant amplitude — a perfect sustained sinusoid. Outside: the output grows exponentially and the filter is useless. Every IIR design tool exists to put your poles where you want them inside the circle.

§ 10

Two dimensions — filtering images

An image is a 2D array of pixel values. A 2D filter is a 2D kernel — typically a small square, like 3×3 or 5×5. Convolution is the same operation as before, just slid in two directions instead of one.

def convolve_2d(img, K):
    # img: H×W image; K: kH×kW kernel; returns same shape
    H, W   = img.shape
    kH, kW = K.shape
    out    = np.zeros_like(img)
    for y in range(H):
        for x in range(W):
            acc = 0
            for dy in range(kH):
                for dx in range(kW):
                    iy = y + dy - kH // 2
                    ix = x + dx - kW // 2
                    if 0 <= iy < H and 0 <= ix < W:
                        acc += K[dy, dx] * img[iy, ix]
            out[y, x] = acc
    return out

Five lines of nested loop, exactly the same multiply-and-accumulate pattern as the 1D case. The kernel itself decides the filter's job. Click each preset:

  • Box blur: a flat square of equal weights summing to 1. Smooths the image — the 2D running average.
  • Gaussian blur: a bell-shaped square. Smoother than box blur with the same support, because the Gaussian's frequency response has no nulls.
  • Sharpen: negative weights around a strong centre. Boosts edges by amplifying anything that doesn't match its neighbours.
  • Sobel-x: positive on the left, negative on the right. A 2D high-pass aimed at vertical edges. The same machinery as the 1D [1, -1] from §2.
  • Laplacian: negative centre, positive surround. A 2D high-pass that detects all edges, regardless of orientation.

The lessons of 1D filtering port over without modification.

§ 11

Choosing your weapon

Eleven sections in, you have everything you need to pick a filter for a job. The rule of thumb fits on a postcard:

  • Need exact linear phase (mastering, measurement, anything where waveform shape matters)? FIR. A symmetric kernel guarantees every frequency passes with the same delay.
  • Need very low latency (live audio, sensors driving real-time visuals)? IIR. Two multiplies per sample, sub-millisecond delay.
  • Need a sharp band-pass or notch at a specific frequency? IIR biquad with high Q. Just remember that high Q rings.
  • Need a wide-band smooth low-pass on cheap hardware (microcontroller, embedded sensor)? One-pole IIR. Smaller than any FIR you could write.
  • Working with images? FIR. 2D recursion is awkward; convolutional kernels are universal and library-supported everywhere.
  • Need to learn the kernel from data (machine-learning style)? FIR. Convnets are stacks of trainable FIRs.

The breath sensor that prompted this companion piece uses one IIR (the user's own one-pole low-pass, on the host) plus, inside its firmware, a 4-section biquad cascade with high Q — exactly the resonant structure §8 modelled. The sensor's apparent "5 second lag" was the same impulse-response ringing we just saw on the screen, paid for in the same currency: high Q, narrow passband, long settling.

Figure 11 · the same operation, four jobshover for code

The four boxes above all do the same thing — multiply past samples by weights, add. They differ only in which samples (the input only, or the output too), how many (a few, or hundreds), and what the weights are. Once you've internalised that, "DSP filter" is a much smaller, friendlier topic than its reputation suggests.

A filter is not a black box. It is six lines of multiply-and-accumulate, and a choice of weights. Everything else — the math, the frequency plots, the cookbook recipes — is documentation for what particular weights do. Once you can write the loop, you own the topic.

What this page assumes

  • All examples are computed live in your browser using single-precision floating-point (with one exception: the FFT-based frequency response uses double-precision JavaScript numbers). Real-world fixed-point implementations will look slightly different — overflow handling, gain staging — but the structural lessons are the same.
  • The audio examples synthesise their own test signals; nothing is loaded from disk. The 4 kHz "noise" tone in §6 is a sine, not actual broadband noise — it makes the attenuation easy to hear.
  • The image in §10 is procedurally generated (gradient + ring + diagonal stripes + speckle noise). Same operations on a photograph would look richer but tell the same story.
  • Section 9's "drag a pole" lets you place one pole; the conjugate is mirrored. Real biquads have two poles and two zeros; we've shown the more interesting half. Wikipedia's Z-transform article has the full story.

Model limits

  • The page treats linear time-invariant filters only. Adaptive filters (Wiener, LMS), nonlinear filters (median, bilateral), and multi-rate systems (decimators, polyphase) are entirely different beasts that share the convolution machinery but break some of its assumptions.
  • The "Q ≈ settling time × ω₀ / 2" rule from §8 is the second-order approximation — multi-section IIR cascades can ring for longer or shorter than this formula predicts, depending on how the sections are tuned.
  • "FIR has linear phase if its kernel is symmetric" is true. "Linear phase is always desirable" is not — in some applications (loudspeaker crossovers, real-time effect chains) the price of linear phase (latency) is worse than the inconvenience of nonlinear phase.
