How a chip the size of a thumbnail measures your breathing through your shirt

A 60 GHz mmWave radar can read chest motion at the millimetre scale because it does not measure how strong the echo is — it measures where in the wave cycle the echo arrives. Here is how that trick works from first principles, exactly how the MR60BHA2 firmware does it inside, and why its tidy breath_phase output lags by five seconds.

§ 1

The scale of the problem

Breathing pushes the chest forward and back by about 4 to 8 millimetres over each three-to-five-second cycle. A heartbeat — the mechanical thump of the cardiac muscle against the chest wall — moves the surface by something between 0.1 and 0.5 millimetres, riding on top of the slower breathing motion at roughly one beat per second.

So our sensor has one job: report a number that follows the front of a moving chest with sub-millimetre accuracy, through a shirt, from a metre away. Drag the sliders to vary the two motions and watch the combined chest position over ten seconds.

The amplitudes here are typical of the clinical mmWave radar literature; your own body, posture, and shirt fabric will shift them. The frequencies are real ranges (breath: 0.1–0.5 Hz; heart: 0.8–2 Hz).

Notice that the heart trace, drawn at the same vertical scale, is so much smaller than the breath trace that it almost vanishes when both are summed. Anything that wants to recover the heartbeat has to dig out a 0.3 mm wobble buried inside a 6 mm motion. That is the dynamic range we are working against.

§ 2

The 60 GHz lever

The MR60BHA2 transmits at 60 gigahertz. The single most important consequence of that choice is the wavelength: about five millimetres in air, the same scale as the motion we want to measure.

We will need this fact several times, so it is worth stating cleanly. If a target moves toward the radar by some distance Δr, the round trip shortens by 2 Δr, and the phase of the returning wave advances by:

Δφ = 4π · Δr / λ where λ is the wavelength of the carrier and Δr is the change in target distance.

Pull the frequency slider. At 60 GHz, one millimetre of chest motion turns the phase by roughly 480 degrees — more than a full rotation. At 5 GHz it would be only 24 degrees, which is well below the noise floor of any practical radar. Phase sensitivity scales with carrier frequency. That is why mmWave is the band of choice for vital-signs sensing.

Notice the second column: λ / 2. That is the round-trip-equivalent of one full wavelength of phase, and it is the limit beyond which phase by itself becomes ambiguous. We will return to it in Section 7.

§ 3

A bare tone is ambiguous

Imagine we just transmit a constant 60 GHz tone and listen for the echo. The echo comes back, faintly, with some delay. We could try to read the delay — but there is no clock to read it against. Every period of the wave looks like every other period. After one cycle (about 17 picoseconds at 60 GHz, or 2.5 mm of round-trip travel) the wave repeats and we cannot tell one cycle from the next.

Watch the figure: the outgoing and returning waves are pure tones. Slide the target. The echo's amplitude shifts a little, its phase shifts more, but neither tells us how far away the target is — only that something is there, and it has moved slightly since the last instant we looked.

To recover absolute range, we need a transmitted signal whose shape changes with time. The fix is so elegant it is the basis for almost every modern automotive and short-range radar.

Figure 3 · single tone, no time referenceslide the target

As you can see, no matter where you place the target, the returning wave is just a delayed copy of the same eternal tone. To turn delay into a number, we have to give time itself a fingerprint.

§ 4

Chirp, mix, beat

A chirp is a tone whose frequency rises smoothly during the pulse — think of a slide whistle going up. The MR60BHA2 sends bursts of these, sweeping by perhaps three or four gigahertz over a fraction of a millisecond. Now the wave is not eternal. Right now it is at one frequency; one microsecond ago it was at a different one.

When the echo returns, its frequency is whatever the chirp WAS some moments ago. The transmitter has already moved on. If we mix the two — multiply them and low-pass — we get a new tone, the beat note, whose frequency is exactly proportional to the round-trip delay, which is exactly proportional to the target's range.

fb = (2 · BW · R) / (c · Tchirp) where BW is the chirp's frequency sweep, R is target range, c is the speed of light, Tchirp is the chirp duration.

The figure shows the time–frequency plot. The orange ramp is what we transmit; the teal ramp, displaced to the right, is the echo from a target at the slider's range. The vertical gap between the two lines is the beat frequency fb. Slide the range and watch the gap grow.

Notice the second readout: range resolution. It is c / (2 · BW), independent of carrier frequency. Wider sweep, finer separation between two close targets — but also a bigger demand on the analog-to-digital chain. The MR60BHA2 sweeps about 3 GHz, giving roughly five centimetres of range resolution. That is more than enough to put a chest into its own range bin.

§ 5

From beat to range bins

If there is one target the beat is a single tone. If there are several, the beat is a sum of tones — one per target — each with a frequency proportional to its range. We can pull them apart with a tool we already have: the Fourier transform.

Take an FFT of one chirp's worth of beat samples. Each output bin corresponds to a small slice of distance. The MR60BHA2 takes a 256-point FFT per chirp; the simulation below uses a 128-point FFT to keep the JavaScript fast, but the picture is identical. After the FFT, "the wall behind the person" lives in one bin, "the body" lives in another, "the chair" lives in a third. We call the result a range profile.

Drag the sliders to add or move targets. The top trace is the time-domain beat (a sum of sines); the bottom trace is its FFT magnitude — a histogram of energy by range. As you move a target, its peak slides along the range axis.

Figure 5 · range FFTthree sliders, three targets

As you can see, the FFT's peaks are the targets. The orange dotted line marks the chest range we will be tracking — bin 14 of 64 in the simulation, about 55 centimetres in this synthetic scene. From here on, we will follow that one bin only.

§ 6

Phase: the secret superpower

Here is the move that makes mmWave vital-signs sensing possible. An FFT bin is not a real number — it is a complex number, a point on the plane. Its magnitude tells us how strong the echo is at that range; its angle is something else entirely.

That angle is a continuous reading of motion within the range cell, finer than the cell itself. The cell is five centimetres wide. The angle resolves down to fractions of a millimetre. As the target moves toward the radar by Δr, the angle rotates by 4π · Δr / λ — the same equation we met in Section 2, now living inside an FFT bin.

Drag the chest position. The dot in the complex plane rotates around the origin. Notice how a tiny motion — far smaller than the range cell — sweeps the dot through a large angle. The cell is for finding the target; the angle is for measuring its motion.

Move slowly: at the limit of the slider's resolution (0.005 mm) you are turning the dot by about 2.4 degrees. That is the regime in which a heartbeat lives. Whatever else the firmware does, it has to extract that angle cleanly, frame after frame.

§ 7

Wrap and unwrap

Phase has a problem. The function that recovers the angle of a complex number — atan2 — only knows the answer modulo 2π. It returns a value in [−π, +π]. Move the chest by more than λ/2 (about 2.5 mm at 60 GHz) between two consecutive frames, and the phase appears to jump backwards by a whole turn.

The fix is phase unwrapping. We track jumps from one frame to the next: any difference larger than +π is corrected by subtracting 2π; any smaller than −π gets 2π added. We accumulate those corrections so the running total stays continuous. As the name implies, it unwraps the phase from the circle onto a line.

Drag the motion amplitude. Below 2.5 mm, the wrapped trace (top) and unwrapped trace (bottom) are identical. Push past it, and the wrapped trace breaks into discontinuities while the unwrapped trace stays smooth — provided the motion does not exceed λ/2 between consecutive samples. That last caveat is why frame rate matters.

Figure 7 · wrap vs. unwrapamplitude slider
raw atan2 (wrapped) unwrapped true chest motion

Notice that if you push the motion amplitude very high and drop the frame rate very low, the unwrap fails — the chest moved more than λ/2 between samples, and there is no way to know it took a long way round versus a short way. Sampling has to be fast enough that motion is unambiguous between frames. That sets the firmware's 20 Hz cadence.

§ 8

Doppler: motion vs. clutter

Most things in a room reflect strongly and do not move: walls, furniture, the radar's own bracket. If we just look at "the strongest range bin" we will lock onto a wall. Our subject — a breathing person — is much fainter than the wall and only distinguishable by the fact that something inside their range cell is moving.

To suppress static reflections, the radar collects 32 chirps in a burst and runs a second FFT — across the chirps, not within them. That axis is called slow time, and the FFT along it is the Doppler FFT. Stationary scatterers concentrate at zero Doppler; anything moving spreads to non-zero bins. The MR60BHA2 picks its breathing target from the range bin with the strongest non-zero Doppler energy.

The figure is a 2D heatmap: range on the horizontal axis, Doppler on the vertical. The bright stripe across the middle is the zero-Doppler clutter. Move the person slider to choose where the body sits in range. Move the breath slider to give them more or less chest motion: the dim spot on either side of the central stripe brightens.

Figure 8 · range-Doppler heatmapplace the person, give them breath

As you can see, the person sits next to the bright clutter stripe but slightly off it — that is the only place a breathing target can show up. The firmware's target-detection routine essentially looks for "highest energy that is not on the central stripe", then commits to that range bin's complex value as the source of phase for the rest of the pipeline.

§ 9

A breath waveform you can play with

Now we have everything we need to construct a synthetic version of the signal that arrives at the firmware's filter stage. We pick a range bin (Section 5), read its phase (Section 6), unwrap it (Section 7), reject the clutter behind it (Section 8). What we get out, converted from radians of phase back to millimetres of displacement, looks unmistakably like breathing — with a small heartbeat ripple riding on top.

The figure shows ten seconds of simulated chest motion. The combined trace is what the firmware sees just before it starts filtering. Notice how the heart ripple is barely visible at typical amplitudes, and how breath irregularity (which is the rule, not the exception) makes the signal anything but a clean sinusoid.

Toggle the noise switch. In real measurements, even after Doppler filtering, there is residual phase noise from the receiver chain, atmospheric jitter, target wobble. That is what the firmware's filters are trying to reject.

Figure 9 · simulated phase waveformbreath, heart, and noise

Listen to the visual rhythm. The breath cycle is the dominant beat your eye finds first. The heart ripple is what your eye finds second, after you stop looking at the obvious. The firmware has to find the heart deterministically, which is harder than it looks.

§ 10

Filtering — and what it costs

Breath sits roughly between 0.1 and 0.5 Hz. Heart sits between 0.8 and 2 Hz. Two band-pass filters, one tuned to each band, would seem to do the job — and they do. The trouble is the price.

A filter's sharpness in the frequency domain is paid for in the time domain. The narrower the passband, the longer the filter rings in response to anything new at its input. Show a sharp band-pass a step, and its output sloshes back and forth for many cycles before settling. That sloshing is the reason the firmware's breath_phase output lags.

Drag the filter's quality factor Q. The frequency response on the left becomes a tighter spike around 0.3 Hz; the impulse response on the right becomes a longer ringing tail. The left view flatters the filter — "look how cleanly it passes only breathing!" The right view tells the truth — "look how long it takes to admit that the input has changed."

settling time ≈ 2 · Q / ω0 Q = quality factor of the band-pass; ω0 = centre frequency in radians/second.

For a band-pass centred at 0.3 Hz (ω0 ≈ 1.9 rad/s), Q = 5 gives about 5.3 seconds of settling. Q = 10 gives 10.6 seconds. This is not an implementation bug — it is a property of any sharp filter at this frequency.

As you can see, the only way to make the filter stop ringing within a second is to make it so wide it admits the heartbeat too. The breath and heart bands sit close enough together that any filter sharp enough to separate them must, by physics, ring for several seconds.

§ 11

Inside the MR60BHA2

So much for principles. Now we can read the MR60BHA2's brain. What follows is the pipeline as recovered from a Ghidra disassembly of firmware version 1.6.12, redrawn as a flowchart and reconstructed in C. (The actual code is hand-written ARM Thumb assembly with software-emulated floating point, since the Andar ADT6101P core has no FPU; the C below is the cleanest equivalent we could write.)

Click each stage to see what it does. The arrows on the diagram light up; the C panel below shows the corresponding code. The whole loop runs at 20 Hz — once every 50 milliseconds, an interrupt service routine signals "fresh frame", the main loop wakes, runs every box you see here in order, and goes back to sleep.

One detail worth pointing at: the on-chip hardware FFT engine at memory-mapped address 0x40022000. Without it, the range FFT (256 points × 32 chirps × 4 antennas = 32768 complex points per frame) would not finish in 50 ms on a no-FPU Cortex-M. With it, range and Doppler FFTs cost a few microseconds each.

§ 12

      
Where the five seconds came from

      
We can now answer the question that started this investigation. The host receives three relevant signals from the radar:

      
    
        
  • total_phase — the first float of every 0x0A13 frame, sent at 20 Hz, no buffering, no filtering between phase extraction and UART. Real-time.
    • 
        
  • breath_phase — the second float of 0x0A13, the output of a 4-section biquad IIR cascade band-pass-tuned for breath. Adds the IIR's settling time, which by Section 10 is several seconds for any filter narrow enough to be clean.
    • 
        
  • breath_rate — the payload of every 0x0A14 frame, computed from a 256-point FFT over a 128-sample shift register of past breath_phase values. The shift register has to fill before the FFT means anything: 128 samples ÷ 20 Hz = 6.4 seconds of buffer-fill latency, every time the system starts or the target is reacquired.
    • 
      

      
The figure makes the difference physical. The top trace is the live total_phase, no smoothing. The middle is breath_phase, dragging behind by the filter's settling. The bottom is the BPM's shift register filling up: the dotted line is "how full the buffer is right now"; until it crosses the right edge, the BPM number is meaningless.

      
Press start. The clock advances. Your firmware sees the same chest motion you saw in earlier sections — but the three downstream views diverge sharply.

§ 13

      
The escape hatch

      
The first float of every 0x0A13 frame — the one the library calls total_phase — has none of these problems. It does not pass through the IIR. It does not feed any shift register. It is the per-frame phase delta, freshly computed and shipped over UART within the same 50 ms window.

      
If we accept that this is a velocity-like signal, not a smoothed displacement, we can do our own filtering on the host with whatever latency budget we can afford. The Arduino sketch in this project does exactly that: a slew limiter to clip phase-unwrap glitches, and a one-pole low-pass to take the edge off, and that is all. Total added latency: about 300 milliseconds.

This is the entire content of breath_demo.ino's signal path, ten lines of C. As you can see, the trick is not to filter better than the firmware. It is to filter somewhere else, where we control the latency budget against the application — a synth where 300 ms feels alive and 5 s feels broken.

      
// breath_demo.ino — what the radar publishes vs. what we publish.
const float ALPHA    = 0.15f;   // 1-pole low-pass coefficient
const float MAX_STEP = 0.70f;   // slew clip, in the same units as total_phase

void loop() {
  if (!mmWave.update(0)) return;
  float total_phase, _, _;
  if (!mmWave.getHeartBreathPhases(total_phase, _, _)) return;

  float bounded = constrain(total_phase,
                              smooth - MAX_STEP, smooth + MAX_STEP);
  smooth = ALPHA * bounded + (1.0f - ALPHA) * smooth;

  publishFloat("hands/mmwave/smooth_fast_breath", smooth);
}

    

  


  

    The chip's output is not wrong — it is honestly slow, by physics. The fix is not to second-guess the firmware's filter design. It is to take the unfiltered signal it already provides and shape it for the application that needs it.

Block diagrams and reconstructed C are inferred from disassembled Thumb-2 functions and call graph; the structure is verified, but two specific numbers are not directly read from the firmware:

    
    
      
  • The exact biquad coefficients of the breath and heart IIRs live at flash offset 0x00011B10, which is past the end of the 42 KB OTA image. Their existence and topology (4-section / 8-section cascade, plus per-section gains) is verified; the centre frequencies and Q values used in the figures are inferred from the band edges of the BPM peak-pick (bins 1–7 for breath, bins 12–30 for heart) and are consistent with — but not proven equal to — values in published literature on biquad-cascade breath/heart separation.
    • 
      
  • The radar's exact range cell width and chirp bandwidth are not directly read from the OTA blob; the figures use 5 cm and 3 GHz respectively, the values published in the MR60BHA2 datasheet. Range FFT length (256), Doppler FFT length (32), and frame rate (20 Hz) are verified from disassembly.

Model limits

    
    
      
  • The "settling time ≈ 2Q/ω0" rule is the second-order approximation. A 4-section biquad cascade can ring longer or shorter depending on how the sections are tuned; we have not characterised the actual ringing of v1.6.12's specific design.
    • 
      
  • Real radar data has phase-unwrap failures, target-bin reselection events, and motion-mode switches that this page does not simulate. The synthetic waveforms are well-behaved; the real ones are messier.
    • 
      
  • The page treats "breath_phase = filtered total_phase". The firmware actually feeds the IIR with the per-frame difference Δφ, not the accumulated phase. The visual consequence is a 90° phase shift, not a magnitude or latency change, and is not material to any conclusion drawn here.

What you can do next

    
    
      
  • Run breath_demo.ino on your own MR60BHA2; capture total_phase alongside breath_phase and compare them on the same time axis. The latency we describe should be visible.
    • 
      
  • Replace the one-pole low-pass with a real band-pass (Butterworth, 0.1–0.5 Hz, second-order) on the host. You will get a cleaner-looking trace at the cost of about half a second of additional latency. That is the same physics the firmware is paying for, just paid by you.
    • 
      
  • If you want absolute certainty about the firmware's IIR coefficients, dump the device's flash via SWD; the relevant region is 0x00010000 to 0x00012000.
